# **Title:

A Christ-Centered Interpretive Framework for Understanding Religious Traditions: Unity, Expression, and Internal Realization**

---

## **Abstract**

This article explores the possibility of interpreting major religious traditions—particularly Islam and Hindu devotional movements such as ISKCON—through a Christ-centered theological lens. It investigates whether alignment with Christ can be understood not merely as explicit verbal acceptance, but as participation in truth, word, and life principles associated with Christ. The work distinguishes between *external declaration* and *internal participation*, proposing that the foundation of Christ may be interpreted as an inward realization rather than an institutional affiliation. At the same time, it critically evaluates the limitations of imposing such a framework on traditions that possess their own self-contained theological identities.

---

## **1. Problem Statement**

The central claim under examination is:

> If Christ represents ultimate truth (Word, Logos, life), then any individual or tradition aligned with truth is, in essence, aligned with Christ—even without explicit acknowledgment.

This leads to a tension:

* Christianity traditionally emphasizes **confession (“accepting with the mouth”)**
* Other traditions emphasize **practice, realization, or submission to divine truth**
* The question becomes:
  **Is verbal acceptance necessary, or is alignment with truth sufficient?**

---

## **2. Conceptual Foundation: Christ as Logos**

In Christian theology, Christ is often understood as:

* The **Word (Logos)**
* The **Truth**
* The **Life principle**

This interpretation allows a broader philosophical reading:

* “Following Christ” = aligning with truth, order, and divine will
* Not limited to institutional Christianity

This creates a **universalizable framework**, but it must be used cautiously.

## **5. Critical Evaluation of the Claim**

### **Strength of Your Perspective**

* Promotes unity across traditions
* Emphasizes lived truth over labels
* Encourages deeper spiritual interpretation

### **Limitations**

* Risks **overwriting the self-understanding of other religions**
* Can become **theologically reductionist**:

  * Reducing Islam and Hinduism into “implicit Christianity”
* May ignore **historical and doctrinal differences**

A more rigorous version of your claim would be:

> There exist structural and ethical parallels across traditions that can be interpreted through a Christ-centered framework, but these do not imply equivalence or hidden identity.
